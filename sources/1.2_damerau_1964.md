# Damerau (1964) — A Technique for Computer Detection and Correction of Spelling Errors

## Citation

> Damerau, F. J. (1964). A technique for computer detection and correction of spelling errors. *Communications of the ACM*, **7**(3), 171–176.
>
> **DOI:** [10.1145/363958.363994](https://doi.org/10.1145/363958.363994)

## Summary

Damerau's paper is the pioneering work on automated spelling correction for computer input systems. It identifies four categories of typographical errors — **insertions (10.6%), deletions (17.1%), transpositions (2.4%), and substitutions (70%)** — based on empirical analysis of 40,000 typed words. The paper proposes an algorithm that leverages character bigram statistics and error-type frequencies to detect and correct spelling mistakes.

**Key contributions:**

- First systematic empirical classification of keyboard typing errors into four categories: insertion, deletion, transposition, and substitution.
- The observation that **~80% of all typos are transpositions of adjacent characters** — a critical insight that later motivated the Damerau-Levenshtein extension.
- A bigram-based statistical correction framework that assigns costs to different error types based on observed frequencies.
- Practical algorithm for spelling correction in real-time typing systems.

## Relevance to Distance Compression Under Probabilistic Perturbation

Damerau's error taxonomy directly informs how **probabilistic perturbation models** should weight different edit operations. The empirical finding that transpositions account for a disproportionate share of errors suggests that any perturbation model grounded in realistic data should assign higher probability to adjacent transpositions. For distance compression, this means that compression strategies can exploit non-uniform perturbation distributions — the most likely perturbations (substitutions, then transpositions) dominate the distance landscape, and compression schemes can prioritize accuracy for high-probability perturbations while tolerating larger errors for rare operations. This asymmetry is central to designing efficient compression under probabilistic perturbation.
